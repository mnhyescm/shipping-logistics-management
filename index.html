Setting the Scene

In 1956, Egypt nationalized the Suez Canal. Within months it was closed by conflict. Two-thirds of Europe’s oil moved through it, so every tanker had to sail around the Cape of Good Hope instead — overnight, tanker demand to the West effectively doubled (Stopford, 2009).

Shipowner Aristotle Onassis had a fleet sitting idle, losing money, only months earlier. The closure made him one of the biggest winners of the decade. Nothing about the world’s demand for oil changed. Only the route did — and that was enough to rewrite a shipping company’s fortunes overnight.

QUICK CHECK

The Scale of Shipping

WHY SHIPPING MATTERS: FOUR NUMBERS

~90%: of world trade by volume moves by sea (UNCTAD, 2024).

12,720 million tonnes: shipped in 2024 (Clarksons Research, 2025, cited in UNCTAD, 2025).

3.6%: of goods’ value is the average sea-freight cost (Stopford, 2009).

100,000+: merchant ships make up the world fleet.

KEY INSIGHT

Why 3.6% changes everything: at that cost, it becomes rational to manufacture in one country and sell in another. This is the foundational fact of globalization, not a side effect of it.

Shipping 7,500 pairs of trainers from the Far East to the UK costs about 24 cents a pair in sea freight. Shipping 15,500 bottles of Scotch whisky from the UK to Japan costs about 11 cents a bottle (Stopford, 2009). Cite the 3.6% figure to this source in exams.

Derived Demand

Demand for shipping does not arise on its own. It is derived from demand for the physical goods that must move from where they are produced to where they are consumed (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010). People do not want ships; they want goods. Ships are demanded only because goods need to move.

Figure 1.1. The derived demand chain, illustrated with crude oil: demand for the ship exists only because demand for the cargo does.

FOUR REASONS COUNTRIES TRADE

Resource gaps: No country has everything it needs.

Cost advantage: Some countries produce a good more cheaply than others.

Variety and quality: Consumers want choices beyond domestic production.

Economies of scale: Producing for a global market lowers the cost per unit.

The Sea Transport System: Three Components

A sea transport system is not a ship. It is three components working together (Steenbrink, 1974, as cited in Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Fixed infrastructure: The permanent, land-based network: ports, container terminals, quays, cranes, warehouses.

Vehicles and units: The ships, feeders, barges, and containers that move through the network.

Organizational systems: The information and coordination layer: terminal operating systems, port community systems, customs.

EXAM TRAP

Never answer “what is a sea transport system” with “ships” alone. A modern terminal, whether Rotterdam, Singapore, or Salalah, combines all three. Remove any one and the system fails, even if the other two work perfectly (Steenbrink, 1974, as cited in Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Actors in the Shipping Business

Cargo owners (demand side): Shippers, traders, importers and exporters who need goods moved.

Ship operators (supply side): Shipowners, liner companies, and charterers who provide capacity.

Intermediaries: Shipbrokers and ship’s agents who connect supply and demand — the shipbroker is the most frequently tested actor.

Industry support: Classification societies, insurers, regulators, port authorities.

No single actor controls the whole journey. Reliability depends on how well they coordinate, not on any one performing well alone.

The Westline and Four Systems of Development

For most of history, trading regions were not linked by sea. Separate economies existed in the Persian Gulf, the Mediterranean, the Baltic, and along the Atlantic coast, connected only by land. From roughly 3000 BC, the center of world trade moved steadily westward, a pattern called the Westline: the Persian Gulf, then Phoenicia, Greece, and Rome, then Venice, the Hanseatic League, the Dutch Republic, Britain, North America, and Japan, each taking the lead in turn (Stopford, 2009). The center has always been moving; there is no reason to assume it has stopped.

The 1950s Revolution: Three Principles

Economies of scale: Bigger ships spread fixed costs over more cargo — cost per tonne falls sharply as size rises.

Better engines: Fuel-efficient diesels cut consumption from roughly 55 to 28 tonnes per day, freeing ships to earn more at sea.

Mechanized handling: Containers and cranes cut port turnaround from around 70 per cent of voyage time to 30 per cent.

These three are interdependent: big ships need fast ports; efficient engines only pay off at sea; mechanized handling enables both. Remove one and modern shipping economics collapses.

The Standard Box: A Short History

Before containers, general cargo moved as break-bulk, loaded by hand. Malcom McLean’s Ideal X carried the first modified truck-trailer bodies in 1956 (these were not yet ISO-standard containers). By the 1960s the industry had settled on the twenty-foot equivalent unit (TEU), an ISO standard still used to measure every container ship built today (Levinson, 2016).

Tramp Shipping and Liner Shipping

Tramp shipping — like a taxi: Hired for the whole journey, price negotiated each time. No fixed route; follows the cargo. Typical of bulk trades. Rates track the Baltic Dry Index.

Liner shipping — like a bus: Fixed route and timetable, published fares, many shippers share the ship. Typical of containerized cargo. Rates track the Shanghai Containerized Freight Index.

Shipping Intensity, Parcel Size, and Trade Elasticity

Shipping intensity is how much seaborne trade an economy generates relative to its size. Bulk-export and manufacturing economies generate far more than service economies of similar GDP, which is why Asia remains the anchor of container trade (UNCTAD, 2025).

WORKED EXAMPLE

It is the cargo size, not the commodity, that picks the ship. The same product can travel two completely different ways.

300 tonnes of steel, UK to West Africa: too small to fill a ship, so it moves in a container on a liner service.

5,000 tonnes of steel, Japan to the USA: large enough to charter a dedicated Panamax bulk carrier (Stopford, 2009; Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

FRAMEWORK: Trade Elasticity · Stopford (2009), Ch. 2

Trade elasticity = % change in sea trade ÷ % change in world GDP.

For every 1% rise in world GDP, sea trade has historically grown by approximately 1.4%.

Sea trade has grown roughly 40% faster than the world economy over the past three decades — this is why shipping is highly cyclical: it magnifies the global business cycle.

A Basic Risk Framework

FOUR CATEGORIES OF SHIPPING RISK

Operational: Breakdowns, delays, accidents, port congestion.

Commercial: Freight rate volatility, counterparty default, contract disputes.

Geopolitical: Conflict, sanctions, chokepoint closures — exactly what hit Onassis in 1956.

Environmental: Weather, climate-driven disruption, emissions regulation.

This framework returns in Chapter 3 (cycle risk), Chapter 6 (chokepoint risk profiles), and Chapter 17 (resilience strategies).

International Maritime Passages

A handful of narrow passages carry a disproportionate share of world trade, and disruption at any one reroutes vessels at real cost.

Suez Canal (Egypt, 1869): Europe–Asia shortcut; approximately 12% to 15% of world trade. Disrupted by the Red Sea crisis, 2023–24 (UNCTAD, 2025).

Strait of Malacca: Around 25% of world trade and nearly half of the world’s seaborne oil, with roughly 94,000 ship transits a year and no viable large-ship alternative.

Strait of Hormuz: Around 20% of the world’s seaborne oil passes through this single strait (UNCTAD, 2025).

Panama Canal (1914, expanded 2016): Atlantic–Pacific link; expanded locks now take Neo-Panamax ships.

Cape of Good Hope: Not a narrow chokepoint, but the standard reroute whenever Suez transit is unreliable, as in 1956 and again from 2023.

Chapter Summary

Shipping moves about 90 per cent of world trade by volume at only 3.6 per cent of goods’ value, the fact that makes globalization economically rational. Demand is derived from four underlying reasons countries trade, and a sea transport system combines fixed infrastructure, vehicles, and organizational systems, run by actors whose coordination determines reliability. The center of world trade has shifted for five thousand years through four broad systems, transformed after 1950 by three interdependent principles: scale, engines, and mechanized handling. Tramp and liner shipping serve cargo through different logic, cargo size rather than commodity decides the ship, and trade elasticity of about 1.4 explains why shipping is so cyclical. A four-part risk framework and a handful of maritime passages, several carrying a fifth or more of world trade on their own, complete the picture this book builds on throughout.

Setting the Scene

In February 2016, the Baltic Dry Index bottomed near 290, an all-time low. Second-hand Capesize bulk carriers sold for a fraction of their 2008 value. By October 2021, the same index hit 5,650. An owner who bought cheap in 2016 and traded through 2021 made extraordinary returns — not from operating skill, but purely from the change in what the ships were worth (Stopford, 2009).

This is the asset play: buy ships when the market is depressed, sell or trade them when it is not. It only works if you understand why rates swing this violently in the first place, which is exactly what this chapter explains.

QUICK CHECK

The Four Interlocking Markets

Shipping is not one market but four, and shipowners manage their position by moving between them (Stopford, 2009).

MARKET WHAT IS TRADED SUPPLY RESPONSE

Second-hand prices tend to lead freight rates: experienced owners bid up ship values before rates actually rise, which is why sale-and-purchase activity is itself an early market signal.

The Ten-Variable Market Model

FRAMEWORK: The Ten-Variable Market Model · Stopford (2009), Lecture 4

Stopford reduces the entire maritime economy to ten variables, five on the demand side and five on the supply side, that meet in the freight market to set the rate.

Demand: the world economy, seaborne commodity trades, average haul, random shocks, transport costs.

Supply: the world fleet, fleet productivity, shipbuilding production, scrapping and losses, freight revenue.

The freight-revenue feedback is what makes the model dynamic: high earnings delay scrapping and reactivate laid-up ships, which then feeds back into supply.

KEY INSIGHT

The model is driven by people and cash, not equations alone. Owners and shippers, not a formula, make the calls. Freight rates and ship prices create the cash-flow pressure that forces action, and decisions ride waves of optimism and pessimism as much as they follow logic. Shipping is often described as resembling a gambling game as much as a transport business (Stopford, 2009).

Five Determinants of Demand

DETERMINANT WHAT IT CAPTURES EXAMPLE

Seaborne commodity trades Which goods are being traded A construction boom lifts ore and steel; a consumer boom lifts containers

WORKED EXAMPLE

The China Effect: the same cargo, shipped over a longer distance, ties up roughly proportionally more shipping capacity, even though the tonnage moved is identical.

Shipping 1 million tonnes of iron ore from Australia to China (~5,500 nm) is the baseline.

The same tonnage from South Africa (~8,000 nm) needs noticeably more ton-miles of capacity.

The same tonnage from Brazil (~11,000 nm) needs roughly double the ton-miles, and therefore roughly double the Capesize demand, of the Australian route (Stopford, 2009).

POLITICAL FACTORS AS A DEMAND DRIVER

Korean War, 1950s: Triggered Western commodity stockpiling — a sharp rise in shipping demand.

Invasion of Kuwait, 1990: Created a tanker boom as speculators used tankers for floating oil storage.

9/11 attacks, 2001: Slowed the global economy, reducing world trade and shipping demand.

The Demand Curve: Movement vs. Shift

Movement along the curve: A change in quantity demanded caused only by a change in the freight rate. As the rate falls, more shipping is demanded, sliding down the same curve.

Shift of the curve: A change in demand itself, caused by any of the five determinants above. An increase in demand shifts the whole curve right; a decrease shifts it left.

EXAM TRAP

Never describe a demand shock as “movement along the curve.” A random shock, a change in average haul, or a shift in world GDP moves the whole curve. Only a change in the freight rate itself produces a movement along it (Stopford, 2009).

Because shipping demand is derived, its elasticity depends on the demand for the underlying goods (Metaxas, 1971; Stopford, 2009). A low transport-cost share, as with microchips, makes demand inelastic: shippers pay a higher rate rather than cut volume. Demand is also more inelastic in the short run, when cargo is urgent and alternatives are few, and more elastic in the long run, once shippers have time to adjust sourcing or routing.

Five Variables That Set Supply

VARIABLE SPEED OF RESPONSE EFFECT ON SUPPLY
World fleet size Very slow (years) A larger fleet means more potential supply
Fleet productivity Fast (weeks) Faster sailing or turnaround raises effective supply
Shipbuilding production Very slow (2–3 years) More deliveries add supply, but only after a long lag
Scrapping and losses Fast (weeks) More scrapping removes supply immediately
Freight revenue (feedback) Immediate High rates delay scrapping and reactivate laid-up ships

ACTIVE VS. AVAILABLE SUPPLY

Active supply: All ships currently trading in the freight market and carrying cargo.

Available supply: Laid-up tonnage — idle but reactivatable once rates cover operating costs.

Total supply: Active plus available. The fleet can appear to expand rapidly in a recovery without a single new ship being built, simply because laid-up vessels return to service.

FRAMEWORK: Rigidity of Supply · Stopford (2009)

Shipping is capital-intensive, so supply cannot flex quickly. Two kinds of rigidity explain why.

Technological rigidity: a new ship takes one to four years to build, and port or canal infrastructure limits how many ships can be used efficiently regardless of demand.

Institutional rigidity: a ship is a 25-year investment costing tens of millions of dollars, so owners only order once a trend looks assured, and scrapping to cut capacity is also costly.

This rigidity is the deep reason a small demand shift can produce a large rate swing.

The Shipping Supply Curve

The supply curve is often described as J-shaped, or kinked, because it behaves very differently depending on how much of the fleet is in use.

Elastic zone: Many ships are laid up. A rise in rates brings them back into service quickly, so supply responds easily and rates barely move.

Inelastic zone: The fleet is fully employed. Only faster steaming or better port efficiency can add supply, so the curve goes nearly vertical and rates can spike sharply, as in the 2021 container boom.

Slow steaming works as a hidden supply lever in the opposite direction: sailing slower cuts an owner’s fuel cost and quietly removes effective capacity from the whole fleet, acting as a self-correcting brake during downturns.

The Freight Rate Mechanism

The freight rate is set where the supply curve and the demand curve intersect (Figure 2.1). At that equilibrium, the quantity shippers want to move exactly equals the quantity carriers are willing to supply, fixing both the rate and the volume traded. Above equilibrium, supply exceeds demand and competition between owners pushes rates down; below it, demand exceeds supply and shippers bid the rate up to secure space.

KEY INSIGHT

Volatility is a structural feature of shipping, not a market failure. Combine inelastic short-run supply with demand exposed to sudden shocks, and violent rate swings are the predictable, mechanical result. The Baltic Dry Index fell to 290 on 10 February 2016, rose to 5,650 in early October 2021, and traded in a range of roughly 2,600 to 3,200 in mid-2026 (Stopford, 2009; Trading Economics, 2026). Different segments use different indices: the BDI for dry bulk, the BDTI and BCTI for tankers, and the SCFI and WCI for containers.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A charterparty allows 4 days (96 hours) laytime to load 40,000 tonnes of grain, at a demurrage rate of USD 12,000 per day. Notice of Readiness is tendered and accepted at 08:00 Monday; laytime begins at 14:00 the same day under a six-hour turn-time clause. Loading finishes at 20:00 Friday.

Laytime allowed: 96 hours. Laytime used, 14:00 Monday to 20:00 Friday: 102 hours.

Time on demurrage: 102 − 96 = 6 hours, or 0.25 of a day.

Demurrage payable: 0.25 × USD 12,000 = USD 3,000, owed by the charterer to the owner.

Chapter Summary

Shipping runs on four interlocking markets, freight, sale and purchase, newbuilding, and demolition, linked by the asset play of buying ships cheap and selling or trading them dear. Stopford’s ten-variable model reduces the whole system to five demand and five supply variables meeting in the freight market. Demand depends on the world economy, trade composition, average haul, random shocks, and transport cost, and a shift in any of these moves the whole demand curve, unlike a rate change, which only moves demand along it. Supply is rigid, both technologically and institutionally, which is why the supply curve is J-shaped and why small demand shifts can produce large rate swings. The freight rate itself is simply where these two curves meet, and chartering allocates the resulting risk between owner and charterer through voyage, time, and bareboat charters and contracts of affreightment.

Chapter 3

Shipping Cycles

CHAPTER ROADMAP

Setting the Scene

In 1956, shipowner Aristotle Onassis had a fleet sitting idle and losing money. When Egypt closed the Suez Canal that October, tanker demand around the Cape doubled overnight, and Onassis, whose ships happened to be available, earned an estimated USD 60–80 million in six months (Stopford, 2009).

His associate Costa Gratsos quietly time-chartered part of the fleet at the top of the market. Onassis refused, believing the boom would last years. In April 1957 the canal reopened and rates collapsed. The lesson was not about luck alone: a manageable balance sheet and better information mattered as much as being in the right place at the right time.

QUICK CHECK

Onassis refused to lock in charters at the peak; his associate did anyway. Whose decision would you defend, and why?

Three Types of Shipping Cycle

A cycle is one completed sequence of recurring events, described by its frequency (length) and amplitude (the peak-to-trough swing) (Stopford, 2009).

Long cycle (30–100+ years): Driven by technology and era shifts — sail to steam to bulk. Relevant to long-run investment thinking, not day-to-day decisions.

Short cycle (5–12 years): Often quoted as “about seven years” but rarely regular. The supply/demand cycle of trough, recovery, peak, and collapse.

Seasonal cycle (12 months): Regular within-year swings, such as more oil moving in the northern winter.

The Four Phases of the Short Cycle

Trough Low, depressed Heavy scrapping; little ordering; lay-up common Depressed — the best buying window
Recovery Rising steadily Laid-up ships reactivated; cautious ordering Beginning to recover
Peak High and volatile Heavy newbuilding ordering; confidence high High, near the cycle maximum
Collapse Falling sharply Orderbook tonnage delivered; scrapping resumes Falling sharply

Figure 3.1. The four phases of the short shipping cycle: ships ordered at the peak arrive two to three years later, after demand has cooled (Stopford, 2009).

FRAMEWORK: The Orderbook Bulge · Stopford (2009)

This is the mechanical engine behind every collapse. Owners order heavily at the peak, when confidence and cash flow are both high, but new ships take two to three years to deliver.

By the time the peak-era orders actually arrive, demand has typically already slowed, so the fleet expands just as it is least needed, driving rates down mechanically, not through any change in sentiment.

This is the same institutional rigidity introduced in Chapter 2: the two-to-three year build lag is exactly what turns an ordinary demand slowdown into a sharp price collapse.

Anatomy of the Short Cycle

Trough: Rates fall to the operating cost of the least efficient ship. Lay-up rises, ships slow-steam and queue, banks foreclose, and distress sales appear. Scrapping here sows the seeds of the next recovery.

Recovery: Supply and demand move toward balance. Rates edge above operating cost, lay-up falls, sentiment stays volatile between optimism and doubt, and second-hand prices begin to rise.

Peak: The fleet runs at full speed and the market is tight. Freight can reach two to three times operating cost, sometimes ten to twenty times. Second-hand prices can exceed newbuilding prices, and ordering runs three to four years ahead of actual need.

Collapse: Triggered when the orderbook bulge lands just as demand weakens. Rates fall fast, and owners who ordered at the peak often face the steepest losses.

Twenty-Two Cycles, 1741–2015 (extending Stopford’s 21-cycle count to include 2007–2015 data)

Measured by long-run freight indices, around twenty-two short cycles have occurred since 1741, across three distinct technology eras (Stopford, 2009; with author’s extension of data to 2015).

1741–1869: the sailing-ship era: Freight rose as sail technology hit its technical and size limits. Cycles tied closely to wars, including the Napoleonic Wars and the US Civil War.

1869–1936: the tramp and liner era: Steam power and cheap coal cut costs sharply. The freight index spiked to roughly 751 in 1918 on wartime shipping losses, then collapsed into the 1930s depression.

1945–today: the bulk era: Tanker and dry-bulk cycles have swung on oil crises, Suez closures, the China-driven commodity boom, and the 2008 credit crisis.

INDUSTRY NOTE

In 1978, Western European and Japanese shipbuilders published forecasts predicting a shipyard capacity recovery to around 21 million CGT of output by the mid-1980s. Each successive revision in 1980, 1982, and 1984 pushed that recovery further into the future, and actual output did not reach the forecast level until 2003, roughly two decades later than first predicted. Over one hundred shipyards closed during the 1980s recession (based on Stopford’s lecture materials). The lesson is not that forecasting is worthless, but that even well-informed industry forecasts have consistently underestimated how long a shipbuilding downturn can run.

Bulk vs. Container Cycles

Bulk shipping: Fragmented and slow to flex. Thousands of small owners compete in a near-perfectly competitive market. The only fast levers are scrapping, lay-up, and slow steaming, and rates ride indices such as the Baltic Dry Index.

Container/liner shipping: Concentrated and actively managed. A small number of large carriers, mostly organized in alliances, can remove capacity quickly and reversibly through blank sailings, letting container rates react faster than bulk rates to changing conditions.

Lessons on Predicting Cycles

SIX COMMON FORECASTING ERRORS

Over-precision: Treating a broad-brush forecast as if it were exact.

Overconfidence: Predicting outcomes in areas the forecaster does not know well.

Status quo bias: Assuming the future will resemble the present.

Correlated assumptions: Copying the same assumptions everyone else is using, because it feels safer.

Across roughly 125 years of shipping history, about 60 per cent of the time has been spent in “competitive” markets, where owners cover costs and make modest profits but must work hard for large ones, and only about 20 per cent in genuine depression (based on Stopford’s historical analysis). The practical takeaway is to focus on market tone, the fundamentals of supply and demand, rather than trying to time an exact peak or trough.

COMMON MISTAKE

Do not write: “shipping cycles last about seven years, so the next peak is predictable.” The seven-year figure is a loose statistical average, not a forecasting rule. Cycle length has varied from about five to over fourteen years across different eras, and treating it as a fixed clock has repeatedly misled investors, as the 1978–88 shipbuilding forecasts show.

Chapter Summary

The short cycle runs through trough, recovery, peak, and collapse, driven mechanically by the orderbook bulge, since ships ordered at the peak arrive two to three years later, just as demand has already softened. Twenty-two such cycles have occurred since 1741 (extending Stopford’s 21-cycle count), and bulk shipping, being fragmented, adjusts more slowly than concentrated, alliance-managed container shipping. The 1956 Suez Crisis and the 1978–88 shipbuilding forecasts both show that cycle timing defeats even experienced, well-informed decision-makers, and the practical response is to watch market tone and avoid the six common forecasting errors rather than trying to call an exact peak or trough.

The Bulk Shipping Market

CHAPTER ROADMAP

  2. Explain the empirical model of bulk fleet size determination.

Setting the Scene

By April 2026, Capesize spot rates had doubled year-on-year, testing the USD 35,000–40,000-per-day range (MaritimeCyprus, 2026), driven by strong Australian iron ore exports and a continued blockade of the Strait of Hormuz that injected volatility into bunker prices and trading conditions. Dry bulk ton-miles rose 5.2 per cent year-on-year in the first quarter of 2026 alone (MaritimeCyprus, 2026). None of this reflected a change in how much iron ore the world produced. It reflected disruption, geography, and a market too fragmented to absorb the shock smoothly.

The Structure of the Bulk Shipping Market

Bulk shipping runs through the same four interlocking markets described in Chapter 2, freight, sale and purchase, newbuilding, and demolition, but it is also one of the most fragmented and competitive shipping segments. Thousands of small, independent owners compete in a near-perfectly competitive market, which is why bulk rates respond quickly to changing fundamentals but the fleet itself adjusts only slowly (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

The Empirical Model of Bulk Fleet Size

Bulk fleet size follows Chapter 2’s ten-variable model, concentrated on a narrower set of commodities. Seaborne trade volume, average haul, and freight rates all feed into how large a bulk fleet the market can sustain, while the two-to-three year shipbuilding lag means the fleet catches up with demand only after a long delay. Supply growth in 2026 was forecast at roughly 2.6 per cent, concentrated in the Panamax and Supramax segments, while Capesize supply remained comparatively contained, giving that segment a more favorable demand balance than the smaller classes (InterManager, 2025; MaritimeCyprus, 2026).

Major and Minor Bulk Trades

Major bulks: Iron ore, coal, grain, bauxite, and phosphate rock — five commodities that dominate bulk tonnage and typically move in the largest parcel sizes.

Minor bulks: Hundreds of smaller trades: steel products, forest products, cement, sugar, fertilizers, and non-ferrous ores, generally shipped in smaller, more varied parcel sizes.

Bulk Carrier Size Classes

Vessel size classes exist because canal and port dimensions are real physical constraints, not marketing categories, and each class earns a different rate for a reason.

CLASS TYPICAL SIZE TYPICAL CARGO CANAL ACCESS
Handysize / Handymax 15,000–60,000 DWT Minor bulks, grain into draft-restricted ports Full flexibility
Supramax / Ultramax 48,000–65,000 DWT Coal, grain, bauxite, minor bulks Panama AND Suez — full global routing
Panamax / Kamsarmax 60,000–82,000 DWT Coal, grain Panama Canal (historic limit)
Capesize 150,000–200,000+ DWT Iron ore, coal, in single large parcels Neither canal — rounds the Cape or Cape Horn

The canal-access column matters commercially, not just technically: a Supramax can transit both the Panama and Suez Canals, giving it routing flexibility a Capesize simply does not have. This is one reason Supramax newbuilding and secondhand demand has stayed strong even as the largest ships dominate headlines.

INDUSTRY NOTE

As of mid-2026, the Baltic Exchange reported Capesize earnings of roughly USD 23,000–23,500 per day, Panamax at about USD 17,000 per day, and Supramax near USD 15,500 per day, with all three segments supported by strong iron ore, coal, and grain demand (Maritime-Hub, 2026). These figures move independently of each other because each class serves a different mix of cargo and routes, which is exactly why the Baltic Dry Index is built from separate Capesize, Panamax, and Supramax sub-indices rather than one blended number.

Parcel Size Distribution in Practice

Parcel size distribution explains why the same broad commodity category needs very different ships. Coal cargoes cluster around two distinct sizes, roughly 66,000 and 155,000 tonnes, reflecting separate thermal-power and steelmaking markets. Iron ore moves in still larger parcels, typically 100,000 to 200,000 tonnes and sometimes beyond 400,000 tonnes. Grain clusters around roughly 25,000 and 60,000 tonnes. Sugar, a minor bulk, clusters around only 27,000 tonnes (based on fixture data analysis from Stopford’s teaching materials, 2010–2015).

WORKED EXAMPLE

A trader is comparing shipping options for two grain lots: 25,000 tonnes and 60,000 tonnes, same commodity, same region.

The 25,000-tonne lot fits a Handysize or Handymax bulk carrier efficiently, matching the smaller of grain’s two typical parcel clusters.

The 60,000-tonne lot is better suited to a larger Panamax vessel, matching the larger cluster.

Chartering a single large vessel to carry only 25,000 tonnes would leave significant unused capacity, raising cost per tonne unnecessarily, exactly the parcel-size logic from Chapter 1.

EXAM TRAP

Never answer a bulk-shipping question by naming only the commodity. Iron ore, coal, and grain each have their own parcel size distribution, and two shipments of the identical commodity can need completely different ships if their parcel sizes differ. Always name the parcel size and, where relevant, the vessel class and its canal access, not just the cargo type.

Geopolitical Risk and Bulk Freight Rates

Because bulk shipping is so fragmented, disruption passes almost directly into freight rates, with none of the buffering that concentrated, alliance-managed container shipping can achieve through blank sailings (see Chapter 5). The 2026 Strait of Hormuz disruption illustrates this directly: ships do not sit idle waiting out a crisis, so time lost to disruption, hesitation, and rapid repositioning is effective supply permanently removed from the market, which is why the conflict acted as a volatility accelerator rather than simply a drag on demand (MaritimeCyprus, 2026).

Bulk shipping operates through the same four interlocking markets as the rest of the industry, but is unusually fragmented, which makes it price-competitive and slow to adjust supply. Major bulks, iron ore, coal, grain, bauxite, and phosphate rock, dominate tonnage, while hundreds of minor bulk trades fill out the rest. Vessel size classes, Handysize through Capesize, are defined by real canal and port constraints, and each earns a different rate because each serves a different mix of cargo and routing flexibility. Parcel size distribution, not commodity type alone, determines which ship a given shipment needs, and because the market is so fragmented, geopolitical disruption such as the 2026 Hormuz crisis passes almost directly into freight rate volatility.

Chapter 5

The Container Shipping Market

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter applies the same demand-and-supply mechanics to container shipping, the segment that behaves most differently from bulk because a handful of large, allied carriers actively manage capacity rather than simply reacting to price.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Explain industrial organization in container shipping.

  2. Apply the empirical model of capacity adjustment to a real 2026 example.

  3. Explain the determinants of fleet size in container shipping.

  4. Explain economies of scale in container ship size.

Setting the Scene

In February 2026, the Shanghai Containerized Freight Index fell to below 990 points, its lowest level in years, as roughly 2.8 million TEU of new capacity, about 15 per cent of the existing fleet, entered service faster than cargo volumes could absorb it. By June 2026, the same index had climbed back above 3,000 as carriers competed for space heading into peak season (Gerudo Logistics, 2026). Nothing about the ships or the ports changed in those four months. The balance between capacity and demand did.

Industrial Organization in Container Shipping

Unlike the fragmented bulk market described in Chapter 4, container shipping is concentrated: a small number of large carriers, most operating within alliances, control most global capacity. This concentration lets carriers manage capacity actively, through blank sailings and network redesign, rather than simply absorbing whatever the market delivers (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Capacity Adjustment: The Empirical Model

Container fleet capacity adjusts through the same forces as any shipping segment, seaborne trade volume, freight rate, and the shipbuilding lag, but the effect of concentration is to make the adjustment faster and more deliberate. Where a fragmented bulk market can only slow-steam or lay up ships passively, a small number of allied container carriers can coordinate blank sailings across a shared network almost immediately (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Determinants of Fleet Size

Applied to the 2026 alliance structure, this model explains a lot: Gemini Cooperation, controlling roughly 20 per cent of global capacity, the Ocean Alliance at about 30 per cent, and the Premier Alliance at about 15 per cent all adjust their own capacity in response to the same demand signals, but their scale means each individual decision moves the market noticeably (Gerudo Logistics, 2026).

Container Ship Economics: TEU, FEU, and Scale

A twenty-foot equivalent unit (TEU) is the standard measure of container capacity; a forty-foot equivalent unit (FEU) equals two TEU. The largest ships in service, such as the MSC Irina class (built 2023), carry up to 24,346 TEU, more than four times a Panamax container ship’s roughly 5,000 TEU limit.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A carrier compares a 10,000 TEU vessel against a 20,000 TEU vessel on the same route, assuming similar utilization.

If fixed voyage costs rise by only 60 per cent when capacity doubles, because larger ships are more fuel-efficient per TEU and need similar crew sizes, cost per TEU falls significantly.

A 10,000 TEU voyage costing USD 1,000,000 (USD 100 per TEU) becomes, at 20,000 TEU and USD 1,600,000, only USD 80 per TEU.

This is the same economies-of-scale logic that has driven container ship size from a few hundred TEU in the 1960s to over 24,000 TEU today: as long as the ship can be filled, bigger genuinely is cheaper per container.

COMMON MISTAKE

Do not write: “bigger ships are always more profitable.” Economies of scale only pay off if the ship can actually be filled and the ports it calls can handle it. An underfilled 24,000 TEU ship can cost more per TEU carried than a fully utilized smaller vessel.

Chapter Summary

Container shipping is concentrated rather than fragmented, which lets a small number of allied carriers manage capacity actively rather than simply reacting to price, as the sharp 2026 swing from a rate collapse to a capacity squeeze demonstrated. Fleet capacity still adjusts through the same underlying demand, freight rate, and shipbuilding-lag forces as any shipping segment, but concentration makes the adjustment faster. Ship size continues to grow because larger vessels lower cost per TEU, provided they can be filled and the ports they call can actually handle them.

Chapter 6

Business Strategy and Growth in Shipping

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter shifts from market mechanics to company decisions: how a shipping firm actually grows and competes. It sets up Chapter 7’s fleet-mix decisions and Chapter 8’s network design, both of which are strategy put into practice.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Distinguish corporate, business, and functional strategy in a shipping company.

  2. Apply market orientation concepts to a shipping firm.

  3. Distinguish operational effectiveness from competitive strategy.

  4. Compare organic growth, acquisitions, joint ventures, and alliances using real 2025–26 examples.

Setting the Scene

In 2026, Hapag-Lloyd agreed to acquire ZIM Integrated Shipping Services in an all-cash deal worth approximately USD 4.2 billion, paying USD 35.00 per share, a 58 per cent premium to ZIM’s prior closing price (on 13 February 2026) (Maritime Professional, 2026). In the same period, CMB.TECH acquired Golden Ocean Group in a share-for-share deal, creating one of the largest diversified listed maritime groups in the world, with a combined fleet exceeding 250 vessels (Conyers, 2026). Two very different growth strategies, an outright acquisition and a share-for-share merger, executed almost simultaneously by different companies solving different strategic problems.

Levels of Strategy

Corporate strategy: Which businesses and markets the company competes in at all — for example, whether a carrier also owns terminals or logistics operations.

Business strategy: How the company competes within a given market — on cost, reliability, or network coverage.

Functional strategy: How individual departments, network planning, pricing, operations, execute the business strategy day to day.

Market Orientation

A market-oriented shipping company organizes itself around customer needs and competitor behavior rather than around internal operating convenience. This means genuine customer focus, active competitor intelligence, and coordination across departments so that network planning and sales are not working from different assumptions about what customers actually want (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Operational Effectiveness vs. Competitive Strategy

Operational effectiveness means doing similar things better than rivals, faster turnaround, lower cost per TEU. Competitive strategy means doing different things, or the same things differently, in a way rivals cannot easily copy. A carrier can be highly efficient and still have no durable competitive advantage if every rival can match that efficiency within a year or two.

EXAM TRAP

Never treat “we are more efficient” as equivalent to “we have a competitive strategy.” Efficiency gains are usually copied quickly. A real competitive strategy rests on something harder to replicate: network reach, a specific customer relationship, or a cost structure rivals cannot match.

Both are legitimate growth strategies. The difference is control: an acquisition ends with one clear buyer; a share-for-share merger ends with a new, shared structure.

Chapter 7

Fleet Mix Decision

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter turns Chapter 6’s strategy concepts into a concrete decision: what fleet should a carrier actually operate? The SCOPE framework here is the direct ancestor of Chapter 8’s network design.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Explain hub-and-spoke liner services and their trade-offs.

  2. Apply the SCOPE framework to a fleet mix decision.

  3. Apply the “Number > Size” empirical ranking to explain performance.

Setting the Scene

The MSC Irina (built 2023) carries 24,346 TEU. On a thin regional route, though, a carrier is often better served by several smaller, more frequent ships than by one giant vessel calling rarely. Fleet mix decisions are not simply “bigger is better” — they are a trade-off the SCOPE framework makes explicit.

Hub-and-Spoke Services

A hub-and-spoke network concentrates large vessels on a small number of major hub ports, with smaller feeder ships distributing cargo to and from secondary ports. This lowers cost per unit on the main haul but adds a transshipment step, and therefore time, to cargo bound for spoke ports.

The SCOPE Framework

FRAMEWORK: SCOPE: Fleet Mix Decision Factors · Lun & Browne (2009), via Lun, Lai, & Cheng (2010)

Service frequency — how often the route needs a sailing to stay commercially attractive to shippers.

Customer value — what shippers on this specific route actually need: speed, reliability, or lowest cost.

Optimal vessel size — the size that best matches available cargo volume and port capability on this route, not the largest ship in the fleet.

Ports of call — how many ports the service realistically needs to visit, and what each one can physically accommodate.

Extensive market coverage — how the route fits into the carrier’s wider network, not just its own profitability in isolation.

SCOPE is why the same carrier operates 24,000 TEU ships on the main Asia-Europe haul and much smaller vessels on regional feeder routes: the optimal vessel size is a function of the specific route’s cargo volume and port capability, not a single fleet-wide target.

“Number > Size”: The Empirical Ranking

FRAMEWORK: Number > Size · Lun & Browne (2009)

An empirical ranking of what actually drives service performance, from strongest to weakest effect.

Number of ships (very strong): more frequent sailings mean more flexibility, better port access, and higher reliability.

Total capacity (strong): enough aggregate capacity to serve demand without chronic space shortages.

Average ship size (moderate): matters for unit cost, but less than having enough ships running often enough.

KEY INSIGHT

This ranking explains a genuinely counter-intuitive result: a carrier running more, smaller ships on a route can outperform a rival running fewer, larger ones with the same total capacity, because frequency and flexibility drive customer-perceived reliability more than raw vessel size does.

COMMON MISTAKE

Do not write: “a bigger ship is always a better fleet decision.” The SCOPE framework and the Number > Size ranking both show that frequency, route fit, and port capability matter more than vessel size alone. A single giant ship calling every three weeks can serve a market worse than four smaller ships calling weekly.

Chapter Summary

Fleet mix decisions are not simply about maximizing vessel size. Hub-and-spoke networks trade lower main-haul cost for an added transshipment step, and the SCOPE framework, service frequency, customer value, optimal vessel size, ports of call, and market coverage, makes explicit that the right vessel size depends on the specific route, not a fleet-wide default. The empirical Number > Size ranking confirms this: how often ships sail matters more to performance than how big they are, which is why real fleets mix ship sizes deliberately rather than standardizing on the largest available vessel.

  2. SCOPE: service frequency, customer value, optimal vessel size, ports of call, extensive market coverage.

  3. “Optimal vessel size” depends on the route’s cargo and ports, not the fleet’s largest ship.

  4. Number of ships beats total capacity beats average ship size, empirically, for service performance.

Chapter 8

Liner Shipping Networks

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter takes Chapter 7’s fleet-mix decision and scales it up to a full network. The same SCOPE logic now applies to a whole map of routes and ports, not a single vessel choice.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Explain liner shipping from a network perspective.

  2. Distinguish types of shipping routes and the role of transshipment.

  3. Explain vessel-sharing agreements and the current alliance structure.

Setting the Scene

When the 2M vessel-sharing agreement between Maersk and MSC ended in January 2025, it did not just change who partnered with whom. It forced a redesign of which ports were called, in what order, and how cargo was transshipped between them across the entire network.

Liner Shipping as a Network

A liner network is not a single route but a connected system: main-haul services between major hub ports, feeder services distributing cargo to and from smaller ports, and transshipment hubs where cargo moves between vessels rather than traveling on one ship for the whole journey (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Types of Shipping Routes

Main-haul route: The long-distance backbone between major regions, e.g. Asia–Europe, run by the largest vessels for the lowest cost per TEU.

Feeder route: Shorter routes connecting smaller ports to a hub, run by smaller vessels suited to those ports’ depth and cargo volume.

Transshipment: Cargo transferred between two different vessels at an intermediate hub port, rather than sailing on one ship the whole way.

A larger main-haul vessel increases the share of total voyage time spent in port: research on mega-ship deployment found that moving from a 4,000 TEU Panamax to an 8,000 TEU super-post-Panamax vessel raised port time from about 17 to 24 per cent of overall voyage time (Lun & Cariou, 2009). This is exactly why hub-and-spoke networks pair large main-haul ships with efficient hub terminals: the economies of scale achieved at sea can otherwise be eroded by diseconomies of scale in port.

Hub-and-Spoke vs. Point-to-Point Design

Hub-and-spoke: Large vessels call a small number of hub ports; feeders distribute cargo onward. Lowers main-haul cost per TEU but adds a transshipment step, and therefore time and handling cost, for cargo bound beyond the hub.

Point-to-point: A vessel calls each port directly on its own route, with no intermediate transshipment. Faster and simpler for the ports actually on the route, but only works where cargo volume justifies a direct call to each one.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A carrier compares serving a secondary port through transshipment at a regional hub versus adding it as a direct call on the main-haul route.

Transshipment adds roughly one to three days of transit time and a separate handling cost at the hub, but lets the main-haul vessel remain large and full on its core route.

A direct call avoids the transshipment delay entirely, but only pays off if that port alone generates enough cargo to justify slowing the main-haul schedule to serve it.

This is the same SCOPE trade-off from Chapter 7, service frequency and ports of call, now applied at the scale of an entire network rather than a single vessel choice.

Chapter 9

The Container Transport Chain: Actors and Incoterms

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter names every actor in the container transport chain and gives you Incoterms, the vocabulary for who bears cost and risk between them. Chapter 10 then builds the physical intermodal chain these actors operate within.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Identify the key actors in the container transport chain.

  2. Explain the roles of transport facilitators and 3PL providers.

  3. Apply the 7Rs of logistics to explain coordination across the chain.

  4. Apply Incoterms to a real shipment scenario.

Setting the Scene

A single container shipment typically passes through the hands of at least six distinct categories of company before it reaches the buyer. Most shippers can name two: the carrier, and maybe the forwarder. The rest of this chapter is about the other four, and about the framework that ties all six together.

Actors in the Container Transport Chain

Primary customers: Buyers and sellers of the goods themselves — the actual parties to the trade.

Freight forwarders: Arrange door-to-door transport on the shipper’s behalf, often consolidating several smaller shipments.

NVOCCs: Non-Vessel Operating Common Carriers — issue their own bills of lading and sell space they book from ocean carriers, without owning ships themselves.

Customs brokers: Handle import and export clearance and documentation.

3PL providers: Third-party logistics companies offering warehousing, distribution, and transport management beyond simple freight forwarding.

Transport operators: Road, rail, inland waterway, and ocean carriers who physically move the container.

The 7Rs of Logistics

FRAMEWORK: The 7Rs of Logistics

Getting the right product, in the right quantity, in the right condition, to the right place, at the right time, to the right customer, at the right cost.

Every actor named in section 9.1 exists to secure one or more of these seven conditions. A forwarder who consolidates cargo efficiently serves “right cost”; a customs broker who clears goods without delay serves “right time.”

The 7Rs are useful precisely because they show why coordination between actors matters more than any single actor’s individual performance: a shipment can satisfy six of the seven and still fail the customer if the seventh is missed.

Incoterms in Practice

Incoterms® 2020, published by the International Chamber of Commerce (2020), define exactly where cost and risk transfer between buyer and seller.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A buyer purchases 500 units on FOB terms from a supplier in Vietnam, shipped to a port in Germany.

Under FOB, the seller’s responsibility ends once the goods are on board the vessel at the Vietnamese port.

From that point, the buyer bears the cost and risk of the ocean voyage, and must arrange its own marine insurance and onward transport from the German port.

If the same shipment had been purchased on CIF terms instead, the seller would have arranged and paid for carriage and insurance to Germany, but risk would still have transferred to the buyer once the goods were on board in Vietnam, not on arrival.

EXAM TRAP

Never treat CIF as meaning “the seller bears the risk until arrival.” Under CIF, the seller pays for carriage and insurance, but risk transfers to the buyer at the same point as FOB: when goods are on board the vessel. Examiners specifically test whether you can separate who pays from who bears risk.

Chapter Summary

A container shipment moves through at least six distinct categories of actor: the buyer and seller themselves, freight forwarders, NVOCCs, customs brokers, 3PL providers, and transport operators. The 7Rs of logistics, right product, quantity, condition, place, time, customer, and cost, explain why coordination between these actors matters more than any single one’s performance, since missing even one of the seven can fail the customer regardless of how well the rest went. Incoterms determine exactly where cost and risk transfer between buyer and seller, and FOB and CIF transfer risk at the same point—on board the vessel—even though they allocate payment for carriage differently.

  4. FOB and CIF transfer risk at the same point—on board the vessel— they differ in who pays for carriage, not in when risk transfers.

Chapter 10

Intermodal Transport: The INTERMODAL Model

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter builds the physical chain that Chapter 9’s actors actually operate within, and gives you the INTERMODAL model, ten named factors that explain why some intermodal systems work and others don’t.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Define intermodal transport and distinguish it from multimodal transport.

  2. Classify the historical intermodal combinations, including TOFC, COFC, and related forms.

Setting the Scene

Hong Kong moves enormous volumes of cargo without owning the hinterland its cargo ultimately serves. What makes its intermodal system work is not any single piece of infrastructure, but ten factors working together, exactly what the INTERMODAL model was built to explain (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Intermodal vs. Multimodal Transport

Intermodal transport: Moves a single sealed unit, almost always a standard container, across two or more transport modes under one contract, without unpacking the cargo at each mode change.

Multimodal transport: Also uses more than one mode, but may involve separate contracts and separate liability for each leg, with no single party accountable for the whole journey.

Classic Intermodal Combinations

Piggyback: TOFC / COFC: Trailer-on-Flatcar and Container-on-Flatcar — road trailers or containers carried on rail flatcars for the long inland leg, then trucked for final delivery.

Roadrailers: Bi-modal trailers with both road wheels and rail wheels, switching directly between road and rail without a separate flatcar.

Fishyback: Truck trailers carried aboard a ship for a coastal or short-sea leg.

Birdyback: Cargo combining air transport with a road or rail leg, typically for high-value, time-sensitive goods.

Land bridge: Cargo crossing a continent by rail between two ocean legs, cutting the total sea distance compared to an all-water route.

The INTERMODAL Model

FRAMEWORK: The INTERMODAL Model · Lun, Lai, and Cheng (2010), building on Lun et al. (2009)

The INTERMODAL model organizes ten critical success factors for intermodal systems. It is not a strict acronym, but a named framework covering:

Infrastructure — e.g. Rotterdam’s Maasvlakte II deep-water expansion.

New technology — e.g. Maersk’s use of RFID tracking.

Transport operators — the road, rail, and ocean carriers physically moving cargo.

External business environment — trade policy, tariffs, and economic conditions.

Regional location — e.g. the Port of Los Angeles’s position relative to its hinterland.

Management of containers — tracking, repositioning, and leasing (see Chapter 11).

Operations of container terminals — berth, yard, and gate efficiency (see Chapter 13).

Deregulation — the extent to which policy allows competition and flexible operations.

Availability of logistics services — e.g. FedEx-style integrated logistics support.

Logistics security — e.g. Amazon-style secure, trackable fulfillment (see Chapter 12).

EXAM TRAP

Never answer an intermodal-systems question by naming only infrastructure. The INTERMODAL model has ten elements, and examiners specifically check whether you can name factors beyond physical assets, such as regulation, technology, and logistics security.

Case: Hong Kong as an Intermodal System

Hong Kong illustrates the INTERMODAL model directly: it combines deep-water infrastructure, an open regulatory environment, strong regional location relative to the Pearl River Delta hinterland, and a dense network of logistics service providers, none of which alone would make it a major intermodal hub, but which together do (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010). (Note: Students should verify current throughput figures, as Hong Kong’s global ranking has shifted in recent years.)

The INTERMODAL model names ten factors, from infrastructure and technology to regulation and logistics security, that together explain why some intermodal systems, such as Hong Kong’s, function well while others with similar physical assets do not.

Chapter 11

Empty Container Management

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter completes the intermodal picture from Chapter 10 by explaining the one problem every container network shares: cargo does not flow evenly in both directions, and someone has to manage the empty boxes left behind.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Explain why empty container repositioning is a major cost item.

  2. Apply the conceptual model of empty container management.

  3. Explain lease types and the box-to-slot ratio.

  4. Distinguish demurrage and detention in the container context.

Setting the Scene

On the Trans-Pacific trade (as of mid-2026), a headhaul rate from Asia to North America has run roughly USD 1,600–1,800 per TEU, while the backhaul rate on the same ship, same route, opposite direction, has run only about USD 700–800. The gap is not a pricing error. It is the market’s way of pulling empty containers back to where the next export cargo is waiting.

Why Empty Container Management Matters

Roughly 46 to 49 per cent of the global container fleet is leased rather than owned outright (sources vary; students should verify current figures), precisely because trade is structurally imbalanced: far more loaded containers move from manufacturing exporters to consuming importers than move in the reverse direction. This leaves large numbers of empty containers stranded at import regions, which someone must reposition, at real cost, back to export markets.

The Conceptual Model

FRAMEWORK: Empty Container Management: Four Elements · Lun, Lai, and Cheng (2010)

Strategic planning — forecasting where empties will accumulate and where they will be needed next.

Procurement — deciding whether to own, lease, or reposition existing units to meet a shortfall.

Movement — the actual physical repositioning of empty containers, the single largest cost driver in this model.

Technical efficiency — maximizing the number of containers moved per repositioning journey and minimizing empty-running distance.

Lease Types and the Box-to-Slot Ratio

Long-term lease: A multi-year commitment, typically lower daily cost, suited to predictable, stable volume.

Short-term / spot lease: Flexible, higher daily cost, suited to seasonal peaks or sudden demand spikes.

Master lease: An ongoing framework agreement allowing flexible pickup and drop-off across multiple depots without a new contract each time.

The box-to-slot ratio compares the number of containers a carrier owns or controls to the number of vessel slots it operates. A ratio above roughly 2.0 to 2.5 generally indicates a carrier is carrying more container inventory than its own fleet needs at any one time, often to support leasing or ensure availability during peak season.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A carrier operates 10,000 vessel slots and controls 24,000 containers, a box-to-slot ratio of 2.4.

This ratio is higher than the roughly 2.0 baseline needed simply to keep its own ships full, accounting for containers in transit, at depots, and awaiting repositioning.

The extra containers above baseline give the carrier flexibility during peak season and let it offer leasing capacity to other shippers, but they also represent real capital tied up in idle equipment.

This is the same trade-off behind the Trans-Pacific headhaul/backhaul rate gap: cheap backhaul rates exist specifically to pull that surplus equipment back to where it is needed, rather than leave it stranded.

ABC Container-Type Prioritization

A: general-purpose: Standard dry containers, the highest priority to reposition quickly since they serve the widest range of cargo.

B: specialized: Reefers, open-tops, and flat-racks, repositioned with medium priority given their narrower use case.

C: rarely-used: Tank containers and other niche types, lowest repositioning priority, often left at a depot until specifically needed.

Demurrage and Detention

Demurrage: Charged while a container sits at the port terminal beyond its free time before pickup.

Detention: Charged once the consignee has taken the container away but has not yet returned it empty.

EXAM TRAP

Never treat demurrage and detention as the same charge. Demurrage applies at the terminal; detention applies once the container has left it. A shipment can incur one, both, or neither, depending on how quickly it clears the terminal and how quickly the empty is returned.

Chapter Summary

Global trade imbalance leaves empty containers stranded away from where the next export cargo needs them, which is why nearly half the world’s container fleet is leased rather than owned, and why backhaul rates on imbalanced trades run far below headhaul rates, a pricing signal designed to pull equipment home. The conceptual model of strategic planning, procurement, movement, and technical efficiency organizes how carriers manage this problem, while the box-to-slot ratio and ABC container-type prioritization determine how much equipment a carrier holds and which containers get repositioned first. Demurrage and detention are distinct charges tied to different locations in the container’s journey, not interchangeable terms for the same delay.

Chapter 12

Container Transport Security

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter explains a question the previous chapters don’t answer: why do shipping companies adopt expensive security technology at all, especially before their competitors do? The answer is rarely pure choice.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Explain why container transport security became a priority.

  2. Describe the main security enhancement technologies in use.

  3. Apply institutional isomorphism to explain security-technology adoption.

Setting the Scene

The terrorist attacks of 11 September 2001 dramatically accelerated the focus on container security, which had previously been concerned primarily with theft and smuggling. A sealed steel box that can carry anything, and that changes hands between six or more actors before delivery, is also a box that can conceal almost anything. The industry’s response was not just better locks. It was a set of new technologies and a surprisingly predictable pattern in who adopted them, and when.

Why Container Security Became a Priority

The same features that make containerization efficient, standardization, minimal handling, and multiple custody transfers, also make a container hard to inspect without slowing the entire chain down. Security measures had to work within the system’s existing speed, not against it (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Security Enhancement Technologies

RFID tracking: Radio-frequency tags let a container’s location and seal status be checked without physically opening it, feeding real-time visibility into the tracking systems referenced in Chapter 10’s INTERMODAL model.

Smart Box Initiative: Industry and government cooperation to build tamper-evidence and tracking directly into container design, rather than adding it afterward.

Non-intrusive inspection: X-ray and gamma-ray scanning that checks a container’s contents without opening it, preserving both security and speed.

Institutional Isomorphism

Why do firms in the same industry often adopt the same security technology at roughly the same time, even when it is expensive and not strictly required? DiMaggio and Powell’s (1983) concept of institutional isomorphism, applied to shipping by Lun, Lai, and Cheng (2010), identifies three distinct pressures.

FRAMEWORK: Three Types of Institutional Isomorphism · DiMaggio & Powell (1983); applied to shipping by Lun, Lai, and Cheng (2010)

Coercive isomorphism — adoption forced by regulation or a powerful customer’s requirement, such as a government mandating scanning at certain ports.

Mimetic isomorphism — adoption by imitation, especially under uncertainty: firms copy competitors seen as successful or legitimate, rather than independently evaluating the technology themselves.

Normative isomorphism — adoption driven by shared professional standards, training, and industry norms, so that firms converge simply because their staff share the same professional expectations.

KEY INSIGHT

This explains a pattern that a purely economic model misses: security adoption often clusters in waves, not because every firm independently concluded the investment was worthwhile, but because regulation, imitation, and professional norms push firms toward the same choice at the same time.

COMMON MISTAKE

Do not write: “companies adopt security technology because it makes economic sense.” This is sometimes true, but institutional isomorphism explains adoption that happens even without a clear individual business case, driven by regulation, imitation, or shared professional norms instead.

Chapter Summary

Container security had to be built to work within the existing speed of the system, which is why RFID tracking, the Smart Box Initiative, and non-intrusive inspection all aim to verify a container without opening it. Adoption of these technologies is not purely an independent economic decision by each firm: institutional isomorphism, coercive, mimetic, and normative, explains why security adoption often clusters across an industry at the same time, driven by regulation, imitation of perceived leaders, and shared professional standards as much as by individual cost-benefit analysis.

Chapter 13

Port Operations and Terminal Facilities

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter moves from the ship and the box to the port itself: the physical facilities and processes that make everything in Chapters 8–12 actually work on the ground.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Distinguish a port from a terminal.

  2. Explain the four levels of port development.

  3. Identify the main facilities and processes within a container terminal.

  4. Explain port and terminal performance indicators, including the CPPI.

Setting the Scene

China’s Yangshan terminal led the 2024 Container Port Performance Index with a score of 146.3. Dakar, Senegal, jumped from a score of −82 in 2023 to +23 in 2024, the largest single-year swing in the index, after crane upgrades and customs digitalization. Neither result depended on being one of the world’s largest ports. Both depended on what this chapter actually covers: facilities, processes, and how well they are run (World Bank & S&P Global Market Intelligence, 2025).

Port vs. Terminal

Port: The overall coastal jurisdiction and harbor area, run by a port authority responsible for the whole site, ship movements, and shared services.

Terminal: A specific facility within a port dedicated to a particular cargo type, container, bulk, or liquid, which may be publicly or privately operated.

The Four Levels of Port Development

FRAMEWORK: Four Levels of Port Development · Stopford (2009)

Level I: a small local port with a basic quayside and a few cranes.

Level II: a multipurpose port serving general cargo ships and a dry bulk terminal.

Level III: a regional port with expanded quayside for larger general cargo ships, small container vessels, and multiple specialized terminals.

Level IV: a regional distribution centre, with large container ships calling and cargo redistributed onward by smaller feeder vessels across the wider region.

A port’s level is not fixed. It reflects accumulated investment in facilities, hinterland connections, and management, which is exactly why Dakar’s 2024 jump was possible without decades of gradual growth.

Container Terminal Facilities

Quay: Where ships dock and ship-to-shore cranes load and discharge containers.

Yard: Where containers are stored and stacked between ship and onward transport.

Container freight station (CFS): Where less-than-container-load cargo is consolidated or deconsolidated.

Gate: Where trucks enter and exit to deliver or collect containers.

Railhead: Where containers transfer to and from rail for inland transport.

Processes and Physical Flows

Cargo moves through a terminal in a defined sequence: assembly at origin, consolidation into full container loads, carriage to the port, and port handling at the quay, yard, and gate. Efficient terminals minimize the time cargo spends between these stages, since delay at any single stage slows the whole operation, exactly the port-time problem that mega-ship deployment worsens, as Chapter 8 showed.

Port and Terminal Performance Indicators

The Container Port Performance Index (CPPI) measures total vessel time in port, from arrival to departure after cargo operations, because this single number captures the combined effect of berth availability, crane productivity, and yard efficiency (World Bank & S&P Global Market Intelligence, 2025).

INDUSTRY NOTE

Between 2020 and 2024, several developing-country ports posted the CPPI’s largest improvements: Posorja, Ecuador, gained 72.8 points after a purpose-built deep-water design; Dakar jumped from −82 to +23 in a single year after crane upgrades and customs digitalization; and Jawaharlal Nehru Port in India rose steadily from 66 to 100 over the same period through expanded capacity and process reform. None of these required being one of the world’s largest ports, only sustained, targeted investment (World Bank & S&P Global Market Intelligence, 2025).

COMMON MISTAKE

Do not write: “the biggest ports are always the best-performing ports.” CPPI data shows mid-sized and even smaller ports can outperform larger ones through targeted investment, while some very large ports lose ground when exposed to chokepoint disruption they cannot control.

Chapter Summary

A port is the overall jurisdiction; a terminal is a specific facility within it, and both can be classified by the four levels of port development, from a small local port through to a full regional distribution centre. Container terminals combine quay, yard, CFS, gate, and railhead facilities, and cargo moves through a defined sequence of assembly, consolidation, carriage, and port handling. The Container Port Performance Index shows that performance depends on investment and management, not port size, as the 2020–2024 gains at Posorja, Dakar, and Jawaharlal Nehru all demonstrate.

Chapter 14

Managing Container Terminals: The PROFIT Framework

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter zooms out from a single terminal’s facilities (Chapter 13) to the companies that now run terminals across dozens of countries, and gives you the named framework for evaluating them.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Trace the development of global container terminal operators.

  2. Explain terminal networks, regional coverage, and internationalization.

  3. Apply the PROFIT framework to evaluate a terminal operator.

Setting the Scene

A handful of global terminal operators, PSA International, DP World, Hutchison Ports, APM Terminals, and COSCO Shipping Ports among them, now run container terminals across dozens of countries. None of them started that way. Understanding why this concentration happened, and how to evaluate it, is what the PROFIT framework was built for.

The Rise of Global Terminal Operators

As shipping lines grew larger and more concentrated (see Chapters 5 and 8), the terminal operators serving them followed the same logic: scale and network reach let an operator offer consistent service and efficient hub connections across an alliance’s entire route structure, not just at one port (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

The PROFIT Framework

FRAMEWORK: PROFIT: Evaluating a Container Terminal Operator · Lun & Cariou (2009)

Productivity — how efficiently the terminal moves containers per hour, per crane, per berth.

Regional coverage — how many ports and regions the operator’s network actually spans.

Operating efficiency — cost per container handled, relative to comparable terminals.

Focus of business — whether the operator specializes narrowly or diversifies across cargo types and services.

Internationalisation — the extent of the operator’s presence beyond its home country or region.

Terminal network — how well the operator’s individual terminals connect into a coordinated system, rather than operating as isolated sites.

Research applying this framework found that efficient global terminal operators are consistently the largest, most internationalized stevedoring companies, not simply the operators with the newest individual equipment (Lun & Cariou, 2009). The distinction between “Productivity” (moves per hour) and “Operating efficiency” (cost per container) is important: a terminal can be highly productive (fast crane operations) but still inefficient if its cost base is too high.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Two terminal operators are compared: Operator A runs one highly automated terminal in a single country. Operator B runs fifteen terminals of varying technology across four continents, coordinated through a shared network.

On productivity alone, Operator A might score higher at its single site.

Applying PROFIT in full, Operator B scores far higher on regional coverage, internationalisation, and terminal network, the three dimensions that let it offer a shipping alliance consistent service across an entire route structure.

This is why global operators like PSA and DP World have grown by acquiring and networking many terminals rather than perfecting one.

Chapter Summary

Global terminal operators grew for the same reason shipping alliances did: scale and network reach serve customers who operate across many ports, not just one. The PROFIT framework, productivity, regional coverage, operating efficiency, focus of business, internationalisation, and terminal network, evaluates an operator across all of these dimensions together, and research applying it finds that the most efficient global operators are the largest, most internationalized ones, not simply those with the best individual terminal.

Agile Ports

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter asks a question Chapter 13’s performance metrics don’t answer: efficient under normal conditions is not the same as resilient when conditions change suddenly. That distinction returns in full in Chapter 17.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Explain why ports need agility, not just efficiency.

  2. Identify the characteristics of an agile port.

  3. Explain the purpose of a structured implementation framework for port agility.

Setting the Scene

A port can lead every efficiency ranking under normal conditions and still fail badly the moment conditions change: a chokepoint closes, an alliance restructures its calls, or a mega-ship arrives with cargo the yard was not planned for. Efficiency and agility are related, but they are not the same capability.

Why Ports Need Agility

An efficient port is optimized for the conditions it usually faces. An agile port can reconfigure quickly when those conditions change unexpectedly, exactly the operating environment ports have faced repeatedly in the 2020s: pandemic disruption, Red Sea rerouting, and rapid alliance restructuring all forced ports to adapt faster than a purely efficiency-focused model anticipated (Lun, Lai, & Cheng, 2010).

Characteristics of an Agile Port

Dedicated infrastructure: Assets the port itself controls and can reconfigure, rather than relying entirely on external parties.

Top management commitment: Leadership genuinely willing to invest in flexibility, not just headline capacity.

Upstream and downstream partnership: Close coordination with shipping lines and inland transport providers, so a disruption on one side can be absorbed by adjusting the other.

Streamlined processes: Simplified, well-documented procedures that can be adapted quickly rather than renegotiated from scratch under pressure.

A Structured Path to Port Agility

Because agility cannot be added to a port overnight, Lun, Lai, and Cheng (2010) propose a structured, multi-step implementation path, moving a port from assessing its current operating environment and uncertainty exposure, through building the four characteristics above, to embedding continuous adaptation as normal practice rather than a one-time project.

KEY INSIGHT

Agility is a capability a port builds deliberately, in stages, not a property that emerges automatically from having modern equipment. A port with excellent cranes and a rigid, centralized decision process can still be far less agile than a smaller port with strong upstream and downstream partnerships.

COMMON MISTAKE

Do not write: “modern equipment makes a port agile.” Equipment supports agility but does not create it. The four characteristics, dedicated infrastructure, management commitment, partnership, and streamlined processes, are organizational, not just technical.

Chapter Summary

Port efficiency and port agility are related but distinct: an efficient port performs well under normal conditions, while an agile port can reconfigure quickly when conditions change suddenly, as ports worldwide were forced to do repeatedly through pandemic disruption and Red Sea rerouting in the 2020s. Four characteristics, dedicated infrastructure, management commitment, upstream and downstream partnership, and streamlined processes, distinguish agile ports, and building agility requires a deliberate, staged implementation path rather than simply investing in modern equipment.

Port Development and Governance

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter closes Part V by explaining who actually owns and controls ports, and the forces reshaping that ownership. The Piraeus case here is a direct test of the governance models it introduces.

Setting the Scene

In 2016, Greece sold a 51 per cent stake (with an option to increase to 67 per cent) in the Piraeus Port Authority to COSCO, China’s state-owned shipping company, for €280.5 million, under a long-term concession agreement. Under COSCO’s management, Piraeus grew from a modest regional port into one of the busiest container ports in the Mediterranean. The same deal that delivered that growth also transferred substantial strategic control over a European Union port to a foreign state-owned enterprise, a trade-off every concession like it involves.

The 4C Forces

FRAMEWORK: The 4C Forces Shaping Port Development · Lun, Lai, and Cheng (2010)

Containerization — the ongoing shift of general cargo into standardized boxes, driving demand for specialized terminal infrastructure.

Concentration — fewer, larger terminal operators and shipping alliances controlling more of the market (see Chapters 8 and 14).

Collaboration — ports, terminal operators, and shipping lines coordinating more closely, often through joint ventures or long-term agreements.

Competition — ports competing directly for cargo, alliance calls, and investment, often against neighboring ports in the same region.

Port Governance Models

MODEL PUBLIC SECTOR ROLE PRIVATE SECTOR ROLE
Service port Owns, manages, and operates most activities Limited role
Tool port Owns infrastructure and equipment May provide labor or services
Landlord port Owns land, plans, and regulates Operates terminals and invests in them
Private port Limited or regulatory role only Owns and operates most assets

The landlord model has become the global standard because it combines public control over strategic land-use decisions with private capital and operational efficiency in terminal operations, typically implemented through long-term concessions like the one at Piraeus.

Port Concessions

A port concession grants a private operator the right to develop, invest in, and operate a port facility for a defined period, often 20 to 50 years, in exchange for agreed payments, investment commitments, and performance obligations.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Apply the 4C forces to the Piraeus concession.

Containerization: COSCO’s investment expanded container-handling capacity significantly.

Concentration: the deal placed a major Mediterranean port under the control of one of the world’s largest, most concentrated shipping and terminal groups.

Collaboration: COSCO integrated Piraeus into its wider shipping network rather than operating it in isolation.

Competition: Piraeus’s growth intensified competition with other Mediterranean transshipment hubs for the same alliance calls.

EXAM TRAP

Never describe a port concession as simply “a lease.” It typically bundles investment obligations, performance targets, and revenue-sharing terms together, which means the granting government is making a long-term strategic bet on the operator’s capability and intentions, not just collecting rent.

Chapter Summary

Port development is shaped by four forces, containerization, concentration, collaboration, and competition, all visible in the Piraeus case. Four governance models, service, tool, landlord, and private, allocate ownership and control differently between public and private sectors, and the landlord model has become dominant because it combines public land-use control with private operational efficiency, usually through a long-term concession. The Piraeus concession shows both sides of this trade-off clearly: real investment and growth, alongside a real transfer of strategic control to a foreign state-owned operator.

Chapter 17

Maritime Supply Chains, Risk, and Resilience

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This chapter finally cashes in the risk framework from Chapter 1, applying it to the whole supply chain rather than a single decision, and gives concrete meaning to the agility concept introduced for ports in Chapter 15.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Define a maritime supply chain and its three flows.

  2. Explain integration and visibility across a supply chain.

  3. Distinguish reliability, agility, and resilience.

  4. Apply the Chapter 1 risk framework to build concrete resilience strategies.

Setting the Scene

On 26 March 2024, the containership Dali lost power and struck the Francis Scott Key Bridge in Baltimore, collapsing it into the harbor and blocking the port’s main shipping channel for approximately three months (Associated Press, 2024). Cargo bound for one of the busiest ports on the US East Coast had to reroute to neighboring ports almost overnight, and those ports, which had nothing to do with the accident, absorbed the resulting congestion.

The Maritime Supply Chain: Three Flows

A maritime supply chain connects shippers, carriers, ports, and inland transport providers through three flows moving simultaneously: the physical flow of cargo, the information flow of bookings, tracking, and customs documents, and the financial flow of payments and insurance (Vanelslander & Sys, Eds., 2020). A shipment can look fine on the physical flow, a ship arriving on time, while quietly failing on the information flow, incorrect documentation, and still arrive delayed or blocked.

Integration and Visibility

Integration is how closely the actors in a supply chain coordinate, sharing data and jointly managing risk rather than optimizing each link alone. Visibility is the ability to see, in near real time, where cargo actually is across every link of the chain, not just the ocean leg. Highly integrated, visible supply chains can absorb a disruption at one link by rerouting elsewhere; poorly integrated ones tend to pass disruption straight through to the customer.

Reliability, Agility, and Resilience

Reliability: Performing as expected under normal conditions, such as consistently meeting a published schedule.

Agility: Responding quickly to short-term changes in demand or capacity (see Chapter 15’s port agility).

Resilience: Absorbing a major disruption and recovering, potentially through a different configuration, rather than simply resuming the original plan once conditions improve.

EXAM TRAP

Never treat a highly reliable supply chain as automatically resilient. A chain built around one fixed route, one key port, or one supplier can be extremely dependable day to day and extremely fragile the moment that single point fails, exactly what the Dali–Baltimore case exposed for shippers who had no alternative East Coast port option.

Ripple Effects

A disruption rarely stays contained to its origin. The Baltimore bridge collision blocked the port’s main channel, forcing vessels to divert to other East Coast ports, which then faced additional congestion themselves, delaying cargo that had nothing to do with the original accident. Companies that had already diversified their East Coast port options absorbed this disruption far more smoothly than those solely dependent on Baltimore.

Concrete Resilience Strategies

Applying the four-category risk framework from Chapter 1 directly:

Operational risk: Diversify ports and carriers so no single accident or breakdown can block the entire chain.

Commercial risk: Hold strategic buffer stock for critical goods to absorb short-term rate or supply shocks.

Geopolitical risk: Build pre-negotiated contingency routing agreements with alternative carriers or ports before a crisis, not during one.

Environmental risk: Invest in visibility systems that reveal weather- or climate-driven disruption early, rather than only after a delivery has already failed.

Chapter Summary

A maritime supply chain moves physical, information, and financial flows together, and integration and visibility determine whether a disruption at one link is absorbed or passed through to the customer. Reliability, agility, and resilience are related but distinct capabilities, and the Baltimore bridge collision showed how a single localized accident can ripple across a regional supply chain within days, hitting companies and ports with no direct connection to the original incident. Building resilience means applying the Chapter 1 risk framework concretely: diversifying against operational risk, buffering against commercial risk, pre-negotiating against geopolitical risk, and building visibility against environmental risk, all before a disruption occurs, not after.

Digitalization and Sustainability in Shipping

CHAPTER ROADMAP

This closing chapter looks forward rather than back, but it depends on nearly everything before it: the ports of Chapters 13–16, the supply chains of Chapter 17, and the risk framework from Chapter 1, all under pressure from technology and climate regulation at once.

Learning Outcomes

  2. Describe the main digital technologies used in modern port and shipping operations.

Setting the Scene

In October 2025, the International Maritime Organization’s Marine Environment Protection Committee met to adopt the Net-Zero Framework, a regulation meant to cap and progressively reduce shipping’s absolute greenhouse gas emissions. Instead, a motion to delay the decision—proposed by Singapore and called to a vote by Saudi Arabia—was passed with 57 votes in favour, 49 against, and 21 abstentions (ShipFinex, 2026). The vote exposed the central tension this chapter is built around: existing measures improve efficiency, but do not yet cap total emissions.

Digitalization and Smart Ports

A smart port uses technology to improve measurable outcomes, faster vessel turnaround, shorter truck queues, lower emissions, better cargo visibility, rather than adopting technology for its own sake. The Port of Rotterdam’s digital strategy, for example, focuses on port-call optimization, using shared data to help vessels arrive exactly when a berth is ready rather than waiting at anchor burning fuel unnecessarily (Port of Rotterdam, 2024–2026).

Port Community System (PCS): Connects a port’s actors, customs, terminal operators, carriers, forwarders, into a single data-sharing platform, replacing fragmented phone and email coordination.

Terminal Operating System (TOS): Manages physical operations within a specific terminal: berth planning, crane assignment, and yard management.

Predictive analytics: Supports ETA prediction, congestion forecasting, and equipment maintenance scheduling, letting ports anticipate problems rather than only react to them.

Digital twin: A continuously updated virtual model of a port or terminal, used to simulate operational changes before implementing them physically.

EEXI and CII

Two IMO measures currently anchor short-term emissions regulation. The Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI) is a technical efficiency standard that ships of 5,000 gross tonnage and above had to meet from January 2023. The Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) is an annual operational rating—also for ships of 5,000 GT and above—pushing ships toward continuous efficiency improvement. Both aim to cut carbon intensity by at least 40 per cent relative to 2008 levels by 2030 (IMO, 2026).

KEY INSIGHT

Carbon intensity (emissions per tonne-mile) and absolute emissions (total emissions regardless of cargo carried) are different measures. A shipping industry that grows its fleet and cargo volume while improving efficiency per tonne-mile can still see total emissions rise, which is exactly the gap the proposed IMO Net-Zero Framework was designed to close.

The IMO Net-Zero Framework

If eventually adopted, the framework would set a Global Fuel Intensity target in two tiers: ships failing to meet the stricter Tier 1 target would owe remedial unit payments of USD 380 per tonne of CO2 equivalent, while ships between Tier 1 and the more lenient Tier 2 target would owe USD 100 per tonne (ShipFinex, 2026). The October 2025 postponement means these costs are not yet in force, but shipowners and charterers are already planning around the likelihood that some version will eventually apply.

COMMON MISTAKE

Do not write: “the EEXI and CII mean shipping’s total emissions are falling.” This confuses efficiency with total impact. Total industry emissions depend on fleet size and trade volume as well as efficiency, which is why a growing fleet can still increase absolute emissions even as individual ships become more efficient.

Chapter Summary

Digitalization improves ports and ships through Port Community Systems, Terminal Operating Systems, predictive analytics, and digital twins, when implemented against clear operational goals rather than adopted for its own sake. Maritime sustainability currently rests on the EEXI and CII, both improving carbon intensity but not capping absolute emissions, a gap the IMO’s proposed Net-Zero Framework was designed to close before its adoption was postponed in October 2025. Digitalization and sustainability are connected: better data and automation are often what make stricter efficiency and emissions targets achievable in practice.
